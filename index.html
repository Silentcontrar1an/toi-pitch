A Delegated Capitation Book Trading at Dispensary Multiples

The Oncology Institute went public via SPAC in November 2021 at a $732 million post-money valuation. Today the stock trades at $3.43 — implying a market cap of roughly $340 million and an enterprise value of $415 million — on a business that generated $502.7 million of revenue in FY2025, posted its first positive EBITDA quarter as a public company in Q4 2025, and guides to $640 million of revenue and $0–9 million of adjusted EBITDA in FY2026. The market has priced this as a clinic on the verge of insolvency. It is not. It is a value-based care operator at the inflection point between negative and positive cash generation, and the market has not updated its model.

The specific misread is structural. TOI operates three revenue streams with completely different economics: a fee-for-service clinic business ($148.5 million, growing 9%), a delegated capitation book ($80.5 million, growing 17%), and a specialty pharmacy dispensary ($269.2 million, growing 50%). The dispensary dominates the revenue line and, at 18–19% gross margins, suppresses every margin-based screening metric. Quantitative factor models see a sub-15% gross margin healthcare services company with a history of EBITDA losses and immediately bucket it with distressed rural hospital systems. They are measuring the wrong thing.

The misclassification is mechanical and specific. TOI holds delegated capitation contracts with Elevance, Humana, and CarePlus across 2.0 million lives under management. Under delegated capitation, TOI receives a fixed per-member-per-month payment — 6–8 times higher than legacy capitation rates — in exchange for bearing full oncology cost risk for a defined patient population. When TOI manages those costs below the contracted PMPM through evidence-based protocols, clinical pathways, and its captive dispensary, it retains 100% of the savings. Less than 5% of contracted lives are under delegated capitation, but that segment generates approximately 33% of total capitation revenue. The sell-side has not published this number. That gap between reported blended capitation revenue and the underlying delegated economics is where the mispricing lives.

VARIANT THESIS I  •  Probability: 65%  •  Valuation Impact: −40–60% to Street PTs

“The Dispensary Is a Drug-Margin Arbitrage Business With a Defined Expiration Date”

What consensus believes: Dispensary is a durable, compounding revenue stream. It grew 49.6% in FY2025 to $269M, will reach ~50% of revenue by 2028 per company guidance, and is treated as a volume story — more patients, more fills, more revenue.

What is actually true: Dispensary is not a patient volume story. It is a drug-margin arbitrage story structurally identical to the 340B model being systematically dismantled at hospital outpatient departments. TOI accesses 340B pricing on oncology drugs at 20–50% discounts to AWP, then bills Medicare and commercial payers at ASP+6%. The spread is the business. That same economics generated $81 billion in 340B program purchases in 2024 — and it is what CMS, HRSA, and drug manufacturers are now actively targeting through three concurrent mechanisms:

First, the HRSA 340B Rebate Model Pilot launched January 1, 2026. It converts upfront discounts to post-sale rebates, creating a cash timing gap. TOI would fund drug purchases at full AWP and wait for rebates against a balance sheet carrying just $33.6M cash and negative FCF. Second, CMS finalized a mandatory drug acquisition cost survey in the 2026 OPPS rule — a direct precursor to reducing the ASP+6% add-on for 340B drugs. Third, per the Community Oncology Alliance, when Part B negotiated prices take effect in 2028, the 6% add-on will be calculated on the lower negotiated price — mechanically shrinking the spread on the exact drugs TOI dispenses most. COA projects a 47% reduction in drug payment revenue to outpatient clinics system-wide.

The math: TOI’s dispensary generates 18.1% gross margin ($48.6M GP on $269M revenue in FY2025). If the 340B acquisition discount compresses 30% through a combination of the rebate model, ASP reduction, and manufacturer policy changes, gross margin on dispensary falls from 18.1% to ~11–12%. On $320M of FY2026 dispensary revenue, that is a $19–22M gross profit reduction — against a company guiding to $0–9M total EBITDA. The profitability inflection disappears entirely.

Why the mispricing exists: Three reasons. The Street has not done the 340B-specific work — it requires understanding a niche regulatory program outside standard oncology training. Management never mentions 340B explicitly in earnings calls; they use “dispensary” as a euphemism. And the rebate pilot is new (January 2026), with ASP compression not hitting until 2028 — beyond the typical sell-side modeling horizon.

That is $40.25 per life per year — or $3.35 PMPM. A true capitated oncology benefit for Medicare Advantage runs $200–500 PMPM per active cancer patient. TOI’s implied rate reveals they are not bearing actuarial risk. They are receiving a per-member management fee for care coordination — a delegated arrangement that looks like capitation but carries none of the margin upside that comes with owning the full oncology benefit. The term “value-based care operator” is doing more work than the contracts support.

The implication is structural: the margin expansion consensus is modeling on capitation requires TOI to win larger, fuller-risk delegated arrangements. Those contracts are competed for by OneOncology, Florida Cancer Specialists, ION Solutions, and the MA plans themselves (Humana, UHC, Elevance) who are increasingly internalizing oncology management. TOI’s largest payer partner is Elevance/Helios — a single-payer concentration risk that is not independently disclosed and not priced into any sell-side model.

Why the mispricing exists: “Value-based care” carries a valuation premium in healthcare services — it signals recurring contracted revenue, aligned incentives, and defensible positioning. The market takes the narrative at face value without doing the per-member arithmetic that reveals a management fee structure. No sell-side note has calculated the implied PMPM. The “fuller economics in 2027” language provides a 12-month runway before the thesis is tested.

“The Physician Shortage Is Not a Risk to TOI — It Is TOI’s Durable Moat, and the Market Has Not Priced It”

What consensus misses: The Street models clinic additions as a capital story. The bears worry about balance sheet availability. Neither side has modeled the actual binding constraint: the oncologist labor market. The 2025 ASCO workforce report found only 4% of oncologists work in high-mortality counties, 38 states had fewer oncologists per capita in 2024 than 2014, and nonmetropolitan areas are projected to meet only 29% of demand for oncologists by 2037. Average starting salary reached $440K–$600K per oncologist in 2023, up 9% in a single cycle.

TOI currently operates 65 consolidated clinics with ~116 oncologists and APPs across California, Florida, Arizona, Nevada, and Oregon — five of the most competitive physician labor markets in the country. Replicating that network from zero would require recruiting 75+ oncologists into those same markets against academic centers, large integrated systems, and well-capitalized groups like Florida Cancer Specialists and OneOncology. It cannot be done in less than a decade.

The bull case from this: Once you understand that TOI’s growth is physician-constrained, not capital-constrained, you also understand that the per-physician productivity curve — not clinic count — is the real value driver. FFS revenue per provider is currently $1.28M, at the bottom of the MGMA $1.2–2.5M range. The upside is not adding the 31 clinics needed for system-level breakeven. The upside is extracting more revenue per provider from the 116 already on staff through dispensary capture rate expansion (40% → 60%) and delegated capitation attach rates. That path requires no capital, no recruitment, and no execution risk beyond operational execution within existing clinics.

Why the mispricing exists: The market re-rates this as a network moat only if TOI demonstrates per-provider productivity leverage rather than net new clinic additions. Management has not framed the story this way. The physician constraint is discussed as a headwind in 10-K risk factors, not as a competitive advantage in investor presentations.

The Dispensary: $27M/Month at 18–19% Margin and Zero Incremental SG&A

The specialty pharmacy dispensary is the most misunderstood asset in the TOI capital structure. At $269.2 million of FY2025 revenue growing at 49.6% year-over-year, it dominates the top line and suppresses every margin metric that quantitative screens use for classification. The result: TOI screens as a low-margin drug distributor when the correct comparison set is a delegated risk-bearing oncology operator with a captive margin enhancement layer.

The operating leverage profile is the correct analytical lens. The dispensary adds drug revenue from patients TOI already sees in its clinics. The incremental SG&A associated with dispensing to an existing patient is essentially zero — the clinical staff, the billing infrastructure, the payer relationships, the real estate are already paid. Each additional dollar of dispensary revenue at the current $27 million per month run rate drops through at 18–19% gross margin with no offsetting fixed cost increase. That is not a distributor business. That is a captive margin layer on top of an existing clinical cost structure.

The IRA Imbruvica concern is an example of consensus pattern-matching rather than actual analysis. Ibrutinib (Imbruvica) is a CLL and MCL treatment negotiated under the IRA drug price framework. Strip the dispensary to gross profit and value the remaining clinical business at 1.5x revenue. The dispensary GP alone justifies a material fraction of the current enterprise value. The market is pricing both at zero.

Delegated Capitation: The Economic Engine at 6–8x Legacy PMPM

TOI's capitation segment reported $80.5 million of FY2025 revenue growing at 17.2%. That number understates the economic significance of the segment because it blends two fundamentally different contract structures: legacy capitation and delegated capitation. Legacy capitation is a low-PMPM arrangement where TOI receives a modest risk fee but bears limited cost responsibility. Delegated capitation is a full-risk arrangement where TOI accepts 100% of oncology cost risk for a defined population in exchange for a PMPM that is 6–8 times the legacy rate.

Fewer than 5% of TOI's contracted lives are under delegated arrangements, but those lives generate approximately 33% of capitation revenue. The Elevance Florida contract is the anchor delegated relationship: currently approximately 70,000 lives, contracted to more than double in 2026. If the Elevance FL book reaches 140,000 delegated lives at current PMPM economics, the annualized capitation revenue contribution from that single contract increases by roughly $50 million — a 62% increase in the capitation segment on a fully contracted basis that requires no new payer wins.

Medicare Advantage: The Structural Tailwind Driving Capitation Demand

Medicare Advantage penetration of total Medicare Part A and B spending reached 54% in 2021 and is projected to reach 62% by 2025. That is not a trend line — it is a structural shift in how oncology costs flow through the US healthcare system. MA payers bear full capitated risk for their enrolled populations. They need delegated oncology partners who can take that risk, manage total cost of care below benchmark, and demonstrate quality outcomes to CMS. TOI is the only independent community oncology network that has built the infrastructure to accept that delegation at scale.

The addressable opportunity within TOI's current Florida and California footprint is substantial. Florida's 2.7 million total MA lives include 665,000 Humana lives for which TOI signed a new contract in Q4 2025 and is only beginning to ramp. California's oncology-specific MA population represents the market where TOI built the CMS EOM PP2 data and has the deepest clinical track record. The geographic concentration is not a risk — it is a distribution advantage. MA penetration in both states continues to grow, expanding the base from which TOI's contracted PMPM flows.

The Bear Case — And Why It Is Overcounted

The bears have one legitimate concern (Deerfield refinancing terms), two overcounted concerns (IRA drug pricing, MA rate cuts), and one valid structural watch (payer concentration). We assign 20% probability to the bear case, not 50%. The asymmetry favors the long.

Bear: Deerfield refinancing forces dilutive equity issuance.

This is the legitimate risk. The $77.4M convertible note matures mid-2027. If TOI cannot refinance on reasonable terms, a dilutive equity raise at unfavorable terms is the bear case. The mitigants are specific: Deerfield holds 3.36 million shares as an aligned equity stakeholder with no economic incentive to impose punitive refinancing terms. The February 2025 amendment — waiving the minimum cash covenant and accepting a partial paydown — demonstrates willingness to negotiate in TOI's interest. At $640 million of revenue and positive EBITDA, TOI is now bankable with conventional credit. We assign 20% probability to a structurally dilutive refinancing and price it at $1.45 in the bear scenario.

Bear: IRA drug price negotiation destroys dispensary economics.

This concern is factually overcounted by 10–20 times. The primary IRA concern repeatedly cited is Imbruvica (ibrutinib). Our analysis places Imbruvica at less than 1% of TOI's total pharmacy revenue. Even a complete formulary elimination of ibrutinib would be immaterial to dispensary gross profit. The IRA negotiates prices for high-volume, high-spend drugs — the oncology portfolio that TOI dispenses is fragmented across hundreds of molecules. No single drug represents a concentration risk at the level the bear case implies.

Bear: CMS MA rate cuts compress capitation economics.

CMS 2026 final MA rates came in below initial proposals. The key distinction: TOI's delegated PMPM is contractually fixed within each contract term. Annual CMS rate adjustments affect what payers receive; they do not automatically pass through to TOI's contracted PMPM rate within the term. The real watch item is contract renewal economics — typically negotiated on 3-year cycles. Short-term CMS rate changes are not the mechanism by which this risk materializes. The bear case is applying a real structural risk on an incorrect transmission pathway.

Bear: TOI is an "Elevance shop" — payer concentration risk is unacceptable.

This was a legitimate concern through Q3 2025. Q4 2025 materially addressed it: new contracts with Humana and CarePlus added approximately 22,000 South Florida MA lives and established a second major payer relationship in the state. Lake Cornelia's Q4 2025 update noted that the Q4 earnings call was "wildly bullish" specifically because the bull's concern about Elevance concentration was directly addressed by the Humana and CarePlus signings. The concentration narrative is declining as a valid thesis risk.

The Deerfield Note: The Risk That Is Smaller Than It Looks

The $77.4 million Deerfield convertible note maturing August 2027 is the most widely cited bear thesis element, and the market is mispricing the probability distribution around it. Deerfield is not a distressed debt fund moving toward enforcement — they are a healthcare specialist that converted a portion of their facility into equity and now holds 3.36 million TOI shares. Their economic outcome is tied to the stock price, not the recovery value of the note. The February 2025 amendment is the clearest signal: Deerfield waived the $40 million minimum cash covenant — a floor the company was approaching — accepted a $20 million partial paydown, and took no interest rate increase, no additional equity compensation, and no accelerated maturity. A creditor optimizing for recovery does not do that. An equity-aligned holder managing through a temporary trough does.

The math: an estimated 40% of treated patients currently fill oncology prescriptions through TOI’s dispensary (inferred from $269M dispensary revenue ÷ ~85,000 estimated patients ÷ $7,921 revenue per filling patient). Each additional 10 percentage points of capture rate adds ~8,500 filling patients × $7,921 per patient × 18.1% dispensary margin = $12.2M of gross profit at approximately zero incremental SG&A. The pharmacy is already staffed, licensed, and operational. Incremental fills are pure margin.

FY2025 provided direct evidence that the capture rate is expanding: dispensary prescription fills grew 66.6% while patient volume grew approximately 18%. The gap — roughly 48 percentage points of fill growth attributable to capture rate rather than patient growth — suggests the capture rate may have moved from approximately 25–30% in FY2024 to approximately 40% in FY2025. The S-curve has not inflected. TOI has not disclosed a ceiling on dispensary capture, and industry comparables for integrated oncology practices suggest 60–70% is achievable over time. The distance from 40% to 60% is worth approximately $24M in annual gross profit at current patient volumes, at zero marginal infrastructure cost.

The blunt conclusion: without the dispensary, TOI is a below-benchmark community oncology practice network with above-market overhead. With the dispensary at increasing capture rates, it is one of the highest revenue-per-patient ambulatory oncology businesses in the US. Dispensary capture rate is the fulcrum on which the entire enterprise value rests. It is also, critically, the variable most directly within management’s control.

A patient worth $2,695 in FFS + capitation revenue is worth $10,616 when the dispensary is included. The dispensary does not just supplement clinical revenue — it triples it. Capture rate expansion from 40% to 60% at current patient volume is worth ~$24M in gross profit at ~$0 incremental SG&A. That alone would take Adj. EBITDA from $0–9M guidance to $24–33M.

The $77.4M Deerfield convertible note matures August 9, 2027. The February 2025 amendment already removed the $40M minimum cash covenant in exchange for a $20M paydown — the balance sheet now carries $33.6M cash against a $77.4M wall that hits in 16 months. In the base case, this is navigable: FCF positive (~$2M), $36M cash at YE2026, approaching EBITDA inflection, and a bankable revenue base. In the bear case, with deeply negative FCF and a dispensary margin collapse, this is an existential event. Deerfield has previously been accommodating — they’re an equity holder — but repeat amendments have a cost.

The one variable that determines success or failure: Dispensary Gross Margin.

A business guiding to $0–9M EBITDA cannot absorb a 600 bps dispensary margin compression without becoming deeply loss-making. The 340B regulatory clock is the threat; the capture rate expansion is the offset. The stock is essentially a bet on which of these two forces moves first.
